For Mike — direct answers to your questions

What devices are we providing, and what do they do?

NVR records every camera + runs the recorder pipeline. Jetson Video Analytics Device drives the spot-decoder UI where the operator marks axle lines. Moxa E2210 handles scale-house I/O signals. 2 × Moxa NPort 5110 bring the serial scale indicators onto the IP network. UPS keeps NVR + Jetson up through power blips. 4 × LIC-CAMERA-CLOUD license cloud upload for 4 cameras. Line-by-line in §2.1 ↓

What will our system do?

Replace AxlEye + the two DataBridge plugins (AxlEye plugin + file-rename plugin). Receive FastWeigh tickets via webhook. Read FDOT permits from the QR camera (no FDOT account needed — public URL). Push our measurement INTO FastWeigh at tare time so the loader operator sees max-weight. Upload every transaction + permit PDF + measurement image to ScaleWatcher Cloud for audit + cross-site visibility Jahna doesn't have today. Software detail in §2.2 ↓

What will it NOT do?

NOT replace the Mettler Toledo DataBridge MS ticketing system — Jahna is doing that themselves with FastWeigh (their separate project). NOT replace FastWeigh itself. NOT do in-motion measurement at Phase 1 — that's Quote C. NOT build a separate permit-registry database. Out-of-scope detail in §3.6 ↓

Is there installation? Who hooks it up?

Quote 17938 does NOT include physical installation. Jahna or their electrician handles rack mount, conduit, electrical outlets, network drops, camera re-aiming — same pattern as how AxlEye was installed. If install scope IS needed, it's a separate billable line. Site walk after PO determines. §3.2 ↓

Cameras — who provides them?

Customer reuses two existing Uniview cameras from the AxlEye setup at every site: side-view IPC2324SR5-ADZK-G + QR-permit IPC3635SB-ADZK-I0. The 4 × cloud licenses on the quote cover cameras whose images we upload to the cloud audit trail. Customer may want other existing cameras (overview, lot, second driver angle, etc.) connected — per-site count firms up at site walk. §3.1 ↓

What are we NOT providing a quote for?

Cameras (customer reuses) · Physical install / conduit / electrical (customer or their electrician) · FastWeigh subscription + credentials (customer arranges with TAC Insight) · FDOT permit access (free public URL, no integration cost) · Mettler Toledo DataBridge MS replacement (Jahna's separate project). §3 ↓

1. The three quote tracks — narrative

The cards above give the at-a-glance view. Customer wants both the manual product (Phase 1, available now via Quote 17938) AND the full AI / in-motion solution (Phase 3), with the AI-assisted intermediate (Phase 2) as a stepping stone that uses Phase 1 frames to train.

Why bundle Phase 1 hardware to support Phase 2 + 3 from day one: the Jetson Orin Nano/NX (JETENGINE-X1 on the quote) is sized so we do NOT have to swap hardware when Phase 2 lands — same compute, same NVR, just a software update. Phase 3 adds a front-of-scale camera + mount + maybe lighting, nothing else.

3.1 Cameras — what we know, what we don't

What we know. Every site we're scoping currently runs AxlEye, which uses two Uniview cameras. Both are reused as-is on our system:

Camera Model What it's for

What we don't know. OPEN Q#5 Each site likely has additional cameras (overview shots, second driver-window angle, inbound-scale view, lot cameras, etc.) that the customer may want connected to the new NVR. We don't have a per-site count yet — that's a site-walk question. Number of cameras directly drives licensing (see next paragraph), so the eventual quote per site can't be locked until we get the list.

Licensing — two tiers, both matter. Each camera connected to our NVR needs:

  1. At minimum, a local NVR license so the NVR can pull and record the feed
  2. In addition, a LIC-CAMERA-CLOUD license ONLY if the customer wants images from that camera attached to truck transactions in the cloud (e.g. an overview shot included with each transaction upload)

So a camera the customer just wants recorded locally needs only #1. A camera they want pictures from on the cloud-side audit trail needs #1 + #2. This decision is per-camera, per-site, and has to come from the customer.

Quote 17938's LIC-CAMERA-CLOUD × 4 is a conservative estimate for the pilot site assuming 4 cloud-uploading cameras. The right number depends on the answer above. Adjusts up or down per site at rollout.

4. What Quote B (AI-assisted measurement / Phase 2) adds

Not in Quote 17938. Scoped here so the customer sees the full roadmap.

4.1 What's delivered

  • AI watches the stationary side-view feed and auto-finds the first and last axle on the truck
  • Auto-counts axles, runs the bridge formula, and fills the measurement in before the operator touches anything
  • Operator's job shifts from "place the lines" to "confirm the AI got it right" — same controller, same display, just less work
  • If the AI isn't sure, the operator places the lines manually exactly like Phase 1

4.2 Hardware delta from Quote A

None. Same Jetson, same cameras, same NVR, same UPS, same controllers, same licenses. Phase 2 is software only.

4.3 Dev hours (NOT in Quote 17938)

Roughly 150–300 hours, billed time-and-materials. Firms up once Phase 1 is in pilot and we have real frames to work with.

4.4 What Quote B does NOT do

  • Does not read axles while the truck is moving — that's Quote C / Phase 3 below
  • Does not remove the operator from the loop — they still confirm or override
  • Does not change anything upstream (FastWeigh integration, permits, portal, etc.)

5. What Quote C (Full AI / in-motion read / Phase 3) adds

Not in Quote 17938 either. This is the "full AI" track the customer asked about. Most ambitious of the three.

5.1 What's delivered

  • A camera at the front of the scale watches the truck approach
  • That single camera tracks, counts, and measures axles/tires as they roll past — providing all the data the bridge formula needs, without the truck having to stop
  • Operator does almost nothing on a good run — just confirms the truck is the right one, the permit scan worked, and the result looks sane
  • When the system isn't confident in its read (unusual truck, weather, glare, off-axis approach), it routes to the operator as an exception. Manual line placement is NOT a fallback here — the side-view camera that Phase 1/2 used for that is gone in Phase 3. Exception-handling mechanism is a Phase 3 design item.

5.2 Hardware delta from Quote B

  • Camera at the front of the scale — either a brand-new install OR the existing side-view measurement camera repositioned to that spot. Per-site site walk decides.
  • Mount + cabling for the front-of-scale camera position
  • Possibly added lighting OPEN Q#10 at the front-of-scale spot (same TX→FL caveat as Quote C open items in § 8 — can't know until on-site after PO)
  • Side-view measurement camera OPEN Q#13 — what happens to it is an open customer-design call (§ 8). Default design retires it (repositioned to the front-of-scale spot or removed) and the Phase 1 / Phase 2 line-placement mechanism goes away with it. Alternative: keep it in place as a fallback for low-confidence in-motion reads (adds dev + hardware retention cost).
  • QR permit camera stays — still needed for permit reading
  • Same Jetson, same NVR — no swaps to the compute or recording stack

5.3 Dev hours (NOT in Quote 17938)

Roughly 250–500 hours, billed time-and-materials. Speculative until Phase 2 is live — the in-motion AI builds on the stationary-AI baseline from Phase 2, so we can't size it precisely until that foundation exists.

5.4 What Quote C does NOT do

  • Doesn't remove the operator entirely — they still own exception cases and the "is this the right truck" confirmation
  • In the default design, doesn't keep manual / AI-line measurement as a fallback — the side-view-camera-with-lines path used in Phase 1 / 2 retires with it. Customer may opt to keep that path as a fallback for low-confidence reads (open call — see § 8).
  • Doesn't replace the QR permit camera (that stays)
  • Doesn't change anything upstream — FastWeigh integration, permits, cloud audit trail all unchanged

Two reasonable answers: (a) clean replacement — no fallback, exceptions go to an operator-only handling flow (cheapest; what the § 5 default describes), or (b) keep the side-view camera + line-placement path in place as a fallback for low-confidence reads (adds dev + hardware retention cost). Customer chooses. Caveat: at least one Jahna site can't physically host the side-view camera, so option (b) isn't universally available even if offered.
Does the operator basically do nothing on good runs, or stay involved as a backup Customer policy call. Changes how the UI is built and how alerts get surfaced.

9. Reliability tier (optional add-on, NOT in Quote 17938)

Jahna's sites get hit by lightning several times per year. Quote 17938 includes a basic UPS but does NOT include the full reliability tier work. We've scoped this separately so the customer can decide. Two tiers exist:

Standard reliability — ~40–80 hr eng + $500–800/site hardware

Properly-sized UPS, surge protection on power + Ethernet + scale serial, heartbeat monitoring + email alerts, pre-imaged USB recovery sticks on every primary device, labeled cables + color-code, printed scale-house runbook, "Something is broken" workflow in dview UI.

Failover reliability — additional ~80–120 hr eng + $300–500/site hardware (above Standard)

Hot-spare Jetson + cold-spare NVR on-site pre-configured for ≤5-min plug-and-play swap by a non-technical scale operator. LTE backup uplink (Cradlepoint or similar, $60/mo/site). Cloud reconciliation worker that sweeps tickets missed during outages (contingent on FastWeigh webhook-replay answer). SMS + PagerDuty alerting with 1-hour engagement SLA. On-site spare kit ($1,500–2,200/site). Annual site visit for surge / grounding / UPS audit.

Why call this out: the resilience design exists because Jahna's lightning-strike profile is real. If the customer wants "ship and forget," the Failover tier is the line item that makes that operationally possible. If they want "Standard," the UPS in Quote 17938 covers a meaningful subset already.
